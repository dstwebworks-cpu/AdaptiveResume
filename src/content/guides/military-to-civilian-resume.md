You did the work. The problem is that a civilian recruiter — and the screening software in front of them — doesn't speak in MOS codes, rank, or unit acronyms. The job of a transition resume isn't to *inflate* what you did. It's to **translate** it into words the hiring side recognizes. Here's how to do that honestly.

## Start with what you actually did, not your title

"Platoon Sergeant, 11B" tells a civilian almost nothing. Before you touch wording, list the real, concrete things you were responsible for:

- How many people did you lead or train?
- What equipment or budget were you accountable for?
- What did you plan, coordinate, or maintain?
- What standards or safety requirements did the job run under?

This list — not your rank — is what you're translating.

## Translate the title

Map your role to the closest **civilian function**, without claiming a job you didn't hold:

- *Platoon Sergeant* → "Operations / team lead, 30–40 personnel"
- *Logistics NCO (92A)* → "Supply &amp; inventory management"
- *Combat Medic (68W)* → "Emergency medical technician / patient care (verify civilian licensure separately)"

> The honest rule: describe the **scope and responsibility** in civilian terms. Don't claim a civilian certification or license you haven't actually earned — that's the one line you never cross.

## Translate the duties

Turn military phrasing into plain, results-oriented civilian language:

- "Led a 40-soldier platoon" → "Managed a 40-person team across daily operations"
- "Accountable for $5M in property" → "Asset &amp; inventory management for $5M of equipment, zero losses"
- "Conducted PMCS" → "Performed scheduled preventive maintenance and inspections"

Notice none of this *adds* anything. It re-states the same true work in language a civilian manager understands.

## Be careful with training and certifications

Military training is a real asset — but only claim what genuinely transfers:

- Training you **completed** → list it plainly ("Hazardous materials handling — completed military certification").
- Skills you **used** but were never formally certified in → describe the experience, not a credential you don't hold.
- Civilian licenses (CDL, medical, electrical) → only if you actually hold the civilian version. Military experience can *help you earn* it; it isn't the same as holding it.

## Quantify — truthfully

Numbers make a resume land, but only real ones. If you genuinely know the figure, use it ("supervised 12 vehicles," "trained 50+ soldiers"). If you don't, leave it qualitative rather than guessing. A true, un-quantified line beats an impressive, invented one.

## Don't over-translate

The opposite mistake is inflation — turning "squad leader" into "senior operations director." Recruiters spot it, and it erodes the trust your service earned you. Translate to the *nearest honest equivalent*, and stop there.
