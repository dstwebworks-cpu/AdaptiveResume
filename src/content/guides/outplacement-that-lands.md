When a layoff sends dozens or hundreds of people to the market at once, outplacement teams face a hard tradeoff: every displaced worker deserves a resume that reflects their real value, but no team can hand-craft them all on the timeline a workforce reduction demands. Consistent, honest enrichment closes that gap.

## The displaced-worker problem

People who've been in a role for years stop noticing what they know. After a layoff — often a stressful, confidence-shaking moment — they undersell themselves further. The resume that results reads thinner than the career behind it. The job isn't to embellish; it's to **recover** the real qualifications they've gone blind to.

## Consistency is the quality lever

Across a cohort, hand-built resumes vary with whoever wrote them. A standard enrichment process gives every participant the same floor: role-relevant skills surfaced, standards and training named, each item confirmed by the person. That consistency is what makes outcomes defensible to your client.

## Keep it honest under pressure

The pressure to "make people look good" is exactly where fabrication creeps in. Hold the line: surface only what the participant confirms, and route any formal credential to a reviewer. A resume that overstates gets the participant into rooms they can't hold — the opposite of good outplacement.

## Quantify the program, not the promise

Report coverage lift (confirmed skills before vs. after) and completion across the cohort. These show your client the program worked without promising any individual a job — an outcome neither you nor anyone else can guarantee.

## Scale without losing the human

Enrichment handles the repetitive surfacing; your coaches spend their limited time where it matters — targeting, interview prep, and the human support a displaced worker actually needs.
